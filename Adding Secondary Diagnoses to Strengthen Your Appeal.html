Adding Secondary Diagnoses to Strengthen Your Appeal

When filing an appeal, adding secondary diagnoses that are linked to your primary conditions can help strengthen your case. Below are the five most common primary conditions and their related secondary diagnoses, which can be added to your claim.

Tinnitus

Tinnitus is often rated at 10% but can be linked to other conditions to enhance your appeal. Consider these secondary diagnoses:

  • Anxiety: Many veterans with tinnitus experience anxiety as a secondary condition, which can be claimed alongside the primary condition.
  • Depression: Mental health conditions such as depression may arise due to long-term hearing problems caused by tinnitus.
  • Sleep Disorders: Veterans may struggle with sleep due to constant ringing in the ears, which can be claimed as a secondary diagnosis.

Limitation of Flexion (Knee)

Veterans with knee injuries may experience the following secondary conditions:

  • Hip Pain: Chronic knee issues can lead to overcompensation in the hip, resulting in pain or dysfunction.
  • Lower Back Pain: Knee injuries can contribute to imbalances that lead to chronic lower back pain.
  • Depression: Limited mobility can result in depression due to a reduced quality of life.

Hearing Loss

Veterans experiencing hearing loss can also file for these related conditions:

  • Balance Issues (Vertigo): Hearing loss, particularly inner ear damage, can cause balance problems.
  • Tinnitus: Often, hearing loss and tinnitus go hand in hand. If not initially claimed together, tinnitus can be added as a secondary condition.
  • Depression: Hearing loss may lead to social isolation and contribute to depression.

Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD)

PTSD is often linked to the following secondary diagnoses:

  • Substance Abuse: Veterans with PTSD may develop substance abuse issues as a coping mechanism.
  • Sleep Apnea: There is increasing recognition that PTSD may contribute to or exacerbate sleep apnea.
  • Anxiety/Depression: Mental health conditions like anxiety and depression are often secondary to PTSD.

Lumbosacral or Cervical Strain

Back and neck strains often lead to the following secondary conditions:

  • Sciatica: Lower back issues may lead to sciatica, a condition where nerve pain radiates down the leg.
  • Radiculopathy: Nerve root compression from spinal strain may result in radiculopathy, causing pain and weakness in the arms or legs.
  • Depression: Chronic pain from these conditions often results in depression.
